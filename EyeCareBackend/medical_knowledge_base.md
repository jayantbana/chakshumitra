Eye Care AI: Master Medical Knowledge Base
SECTION 1: GLOBAL RED FLAGS (URGENT TRIAGE)
Before attempting diagnosis, check for these emergency warning signs found across all protocols.

IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY (Go to ER/Ophthalmologist NOW):

Vision Loss: Sudden loss of vision or marked decline in visual acuity.

Severe Pain: True eye pain (not just irritation) prevents sleep or is accompanied by headache/nausea.

Trauma: History of significant eye trauma (hit by ball, object) or chemical splash.

Structural Damage: Irregular pupil shape, blood inside the front of the eye (Hyphema), or globe rupture.

Spreading Infection: Eyelid swelling extending to the cheek, eyebrow, or other eye (Preseptal/Orbital Cellulitis).

Systemic Symptoms: Fever >100.4°F (38°C) with eyelid swelling.

Double Vision: Seeing two images instead of one (suggests muscle involvement).

URGENT (See Doctor within 24-48 Hours):

Photophobia: Moderate to severe sensitivity to light.

Contact Lens Wearers: Any red eye or eyelid infection in a contact lens user is high risk for bacterial keratitis.

Worsening Symptoms: Conditions getting worse despite 3-5 days of home care.

SECTION 2: DIAGNOSTIC PROTOCOLS (LOGIC MAPS)
PROTOCOL A: The Red Eye (Conjunctivitis vs. SCH)
Condition 1: Subconjunctival Hemorrhage (SCH)
Key Identifier: Bright red, well-defined patch on the white of the eye.

Pain: Painless (mild scratchiness is okay).

Vision: Normal/Unchanged.

Discharge: None.

Causes: Trauma, rubbing, heavy lifting, coughing/sneezing (Valsalva), blood thinners, hypertension.

Management: Observation (no treatment needed). Cold compress for first 48 hours, then warm. Resolves in 1-3 weeks.

Condition 2: Conjunctivitis (Pink Eye)
Key Identifier: Diffuse redness involving the whole eye/eyelids.

Pain: Discomfort, grittiness ("sand in eye"), or itching—but NO true deep pain.

Differentiation by Discharge:

Thick Yellow/Green + Sticky Eyelids: Bacterial (Needs doctor/antibiotics).

Clear/Watery + Gritty Feeling: Viral (Often starts in one eye, spreads to other).

Clear/Stringy + Intense Itch: Allergic (Usually both eyes, history of allergies).

Management:

Bacterial: Antibiotic drops (consult doctor).

Viral/Allergic: Cold compresses, artificial tears, hygiene. Highly contagious (except allergic).

PROTOCOL B: The Eyelid Lump (Stye vs. Chalazion)
Condition 3: Stye (Hordeolum)
Key Identifier: Red, painful bump at the eyelid edge (eyelash follicle).

Onset: Rapid (1-3 days).

Symptoms: Localized swelling, tenderness, may have a yellow pus head.

Management: Warm compresses (10-15 mins, 4-6 times daily) are the primary treatment.

Warning: Do NOT squeeze or pop.

Condition 4: Chalazion
Key Identifier: Firm, painless (or mildly tender) lump further back in the eyelid.

Onset: Slow (weeks to months).

Cause: Blocked oil gland (meibomian gland), not an active infection.

Management: Warm compresses (may take 4-6 weeks to resolve). If persistent >1 month, see ophthalmologist.

PROTOCOL C: The Surface Growth
Condition 5: Pterygium & Pinguecula
Key Identifier: Fleshy growth or yellow-white deposit on the white of the eye (usually nasal side).

Pinguecula: Yellow-white deposit, does not cross onto the cornea.

Pterygium: Triangular fleshy tissue that invades the cornea (black part of eye).

Risk Factors: Chronic UV exposure (sun), wind, dust ("Surfer's Eye").

Management: UV protection (sunglasses), lubricating drops. Surgery only if it threatens vision or causes severe irritation.

SECTION 3: DETAILED MEDICAL CONTEXT (REFERENCE)
1. Subconjunctival Hemorrhage (SCH)
Pathophysiology: Rupture of fragile conjunctival blood vessels. Blood is trapped between conjunctiva and sclera.

Healing Timeline: Changes color like a bruise (Red -> Orange -> Yellow -> White). Takes 1-3 weeks.

Recurrence: If frequent, check for Hypertension or bleeding disorders.

Home Care: Artificial tears for irritation. Avoid NSAIDs (Aspirin/Ibuprofen) as they may worsen bleeding.

2. Conjunctivitis
Viral: Often associated with a recent cold/upper respiratory infection. Contagious for 7-14 days.

Bacterial: "Glued" eyes in the morning is a strong indicator. Resolves faster with drops.

Allergic: Hallmark is intense itching and both eyes affected simultaneously.

3. Stye & Chalazion
Mechanism: Stye is an acute bacterial infection (Staph). Chalazion is a chronic sterile granuloma (blocked oil).

Hygiene: Associated with Blepharitis or Rosacea. Use lid scrubs/baby shampoo to prevent recurrence.

When to Refer: If the lump causes astigmatism (blurry vision), loss of eyelashes, or bleeds/ulcerates (rule out tumor).

4. Pterygium
Progression: Can induce astigmatism (distorted vision) if it pulls on the cornea.

Prevention: Wraparound sunglasses and hats are the most effective prevention.

Differential Diagnosis: Must distinguish from Ocular Surface Squamous Neoplasia (OSSN) if rapid growth or pigmentation is present.
